Start with the model, not the body

This is a supporting page. For the five phases themselves and the generating and restraining cycles, begin with the Wu Xing foundations guide. Here the focus is narrower: the historical correspondence language used in Chinese medical literature.

Wu Xing 五行 (wǔxíng) is translated here as Five Phases. “Five Processes,” “Five Movements,” and “Five Elements” also appear in English. The best rendering depends on the source and what the term is doing in that passage.

The safety boundary

Educational and historical—not medical guidanceThis page does not provide diagnosis, treatment, prevention, dosage, efficacy claims or a method for interpreting symptoms. A traditional “Liver,” “Heart,” “Spleen,” “Lung,” or “Kidney” system is not interchangeable with a modern anatomical organ, laboratory result or medical condition. For health concerns, use qualified medical care.

The correspondence table below tells you how categories are arranged inside a traditional textual system. It does not show that an emotion produces disease, that a symptom reveals a phase imbalance, or that a colour, food, chart or product changes health outcomes.

What “organ” means in this context

English translations often use familiar anatomical nouns for the traditional categories called zang and fu. That convenience can mislead. In classical literature, these terms participate in networks of storage, transformation, movement, sensation, climate, season and emotion. Their boundaries were defined within premodern Chinese medical thought, not by modern anatomy, pathology or molecular biology.

Zang

Commonly rendered as “yin organs” or “storing systems.” The Suwen describes the five zang through the language of storing essence and qi.

Fu

Commonly rendered as “yang organs” or “transmitting and transforming systems.” The same textual tradition contrasts them with zang through passage and transformation.

Modern anatomy

A different knowledge system based on anatomical structures, physiology, pathology, measurement and clinical evidence. Similar English names do not make the categories equivalent.

A layered historical system, not one frozen chart

The received Huangdi Neijing is a composite tradition that developed across centuries. Its chapters preserve related but not always identical taxonomies. Later commentaries, teaching lineages and modern textbooks made many correspondences more regular and easier to display in a five-column table.

One Suwen passage correlates Wood with east, wind, the Liver, sour flavour and anger; Fire with south, heat, the Heart, bitter flavour and joy; Earth with center, dampness, the Spleen, sweet flavour and thoughtfulness; Metal with west, dryness, the Lungs, pungent flavour and sorrow; and Water with north, cold, the Kidneys, salty flavour and fear.

Source methodThe table distinguishes direct classical correspondences from later teaching syntheses. Five rows, five historical vocabularies

Wood · 木 mù

Wood is linked with east, wind, sour flavour, the Liver system and anger. In this taxonomy, anger is a correspondence term—not evidence that anger has identified a liver disorder.

Fire · 火 huǒ

Fire is linked with south, heat, bitter flavour, the Heart system and joy. Later zang-fu tables pair Heart with Small Intestine; some expanded medical systems also discuss Pericardium and Triple Burner, but that six-system elaboration is outside this introductory matrix.

Earth · 土 tǔ

Earth is linked with center, dampness, sweet flavour, the Spleen system and thoughtfulness. “Spleen” here is especially prone to false biomedical equivalence: the traditional category is broader than the anatomical spleen.

Metal · 金 jīn

Metal is linked with west, dryness, pungent flavour, the Lung system and a term translated as sorrow, grief or worry. Translation choice changes tone; it does not create a diagnostic scale.

Water · 水 shuǐ

Water is linked with north, cold, salty flavour, the Kidney system and fear. The traditional Kidney system must not be used as a substitute for renal anatomy, kidney function tests or a medical assessment.

Why translations and tables vary

“Five Phases” versus “Five Elements”

Five Phases emphasizes change and relation. Five Elements remains historically widespread and can be appropriate in some contexts. This page uses “Five Phases” without pretending one English word resolves every historical use of xing.

Emotion words

Chinese terms are compressed differently by translators. Metal’s emotional term may appear as sorrow, grief or worry; Earth’s as thoughtfulness, pensiveness, rumination or worry. These are translation families, not standardized psychiatric categories.

Earth and the seasons

The classical passage used here places Earth at the center. Later teaching systems may assign it to late summer or to transitional periods between seasons. That is a documented variant, not an error to be silently flattened.

Pairs and expansions

The five-row Liver/Gallbladder through Kidney/Bladder table is a common teaching synthesis. Other medical frameworks add further distinctions, including Pericardium and Triple Burner within Fire-related arrangements. This page keeps the introductory five-row structure and names the boundary.

What this model cannot establish

  • It cannot identify a disease, deficiency or “organ imbalance” from an emotion, colour preference, season, taste or personality description.
  • It cannot replace physical examination, laboratory testing, imaging, mental-health assessment or other evidence-based care.
  • It does not demonstrate that a traditional organ category maps to one modern organ, hormone, neural circuit, microbiome pattern or biochemical pathway.
  • It does not support treatment, prevention, supplement, herb, food, acupuncture, music or lifestyle prescriptions on this page.
  • It does not turn a BaZi or Wu Xing chart into a health assessment.

Key takeaways

  • Wu Xing organ correspondences belong to a historical correlative system.
  • Zang-fu terms are not one-to-one equivalents of modern anatomical organs.
  • Seasons, directions and emotions form linked classification rows, not diagnostic tests.
  • Translation and lineage differences should be visible rather than silently standardized.
  • For the phase cycles themselves, return to the Wu Xing foundations guide.

Glossary

Wu Xing 五行
wǔxíng; a fivefold framework translated here as Five Phases.
Zang
A traditional category often rendered as storing, yin or solid organ systems. The English organ name is not a full definition.
Fu
A traditional category often rendered as transmitting, transforming, yang or hollow organ systems.
Correspondence
A patterned association within a classification system. Correspondence alone does not establish modern biological causation.
Huangdi Neijing
A foundational, composite corpus of Chinese medical writing. The Suwen and Lingshu contain multiple historical layers.
